# LMQ vs equal-mass quantization — why we don't just sort and slice

## The naive alternative

Given 34.8B voxel densities ρ and a vocabulary of K = 16384 bins, a tempting
algorithm is:

1. Sort all voxel densities into a giant cumulative histogram.
2. Place K-1 boundaries at the i/K-th quantiles, so each bin contains
   exactly N/K voxels.
3. Use the value at each quantile as the bin's reconstruction point.

This is **equal-mass** (a.k.a. equal-frequency) quantization. It's simple,
easy to compute, and has one nice property: every bin sees the same number
of training samples — which seems like it should make life easy for the
model.

## Why we use Lloyd-Max instead

Equal-mass is **entropy-optimal** (each bin has the same probability mass,
so the discrete distribution maximizes Shannon entropy = log K bits). But
that's not what we want.

For our use case (predict density values, evaluate by NMAE on the
reconstructed-density), we want **low reconstruction error**. The relevant
optima are different:

| objective | optimal bin density |
|---|---|
| entropy (≈ equal-mass) | ∝ p(x) |
| L_1 (MAE) | ∝ p(x)^{1/2} |
| **L_2 (MSE) — what Lloyd-Max optimizes** | ∝ p(x)^{1/3} |

So the L_2-optimal partition has **fewer** bins in the dense regions of
p(x) than equal-mass does — counterintuitive at first, but it's the
asymptotic optimum (Bennett's formula, 1948).

Lloyd-Max iterates two updates to convergence:

- **E-step**: assign each fine-grid sample to its nearest reconstruction point.
- **M-step**: move each reconstruction point to the conditional mean of its
  assigned samples; place boundaries at midpoints of adjacent recon points.

This is locally L_2-optimal. Not globally optimal in the strictest sense
(it's the same local-optimum-not-global story as k-means), but for smooth
1D distributions the local optimum is near-global, and Lloyd-Max converges
quickly (~10-30 iterations on our codec).

## Empirical comparison

Comparing all three schemes on the 16384-bin LMQ codec's empirical distribution:

| scheme | RMSE | MAE | MSE vs LMQ |
|---|---|---|---|
| **LMQ (Lloyd-Max + mean centroids)** | **13.24** | **0.327** | 1.0× |
| equal-mass + mean centroids | 37.98 | 0.752 | 8.23× worse |
| equal-mass + quantile centroids (the "naive" sort-and-slice) | 34.47 | 0.688 | 6.77× worse |

LMQ wins on both MSE and MAE by a wide margin.

The per-bin mass distribution under LMQ confirms it's nowhere near
equal-mass: the heaviest bin has 16.6× more mass than the lightest, and
13.4× more than the equal-mass baseline of 1/K = 6.1×10⁻⁵.

![Quantization error per density-decade](../plots/lmq/03_quant_error.png)

LMQ achieves uniformly lower per-density-decade RMSE; the gap is largest
in the dense low-density region (10⁻³ to 10¹) where most voxels live.

## Why LMQ's local optimum still beats global equal-mass

A useful intuition: equal-mass over-allocates bins to the dense interior
of the distribution. Each interior bin has lots of samples, but they're
already squeezed into a narrow interval, so adding more bins there only
buys you marginal MSE reduction. Meanwhile, the sparse tails get few bins
that have to cover wide intervals — so any sample landing there has high
quantization noise, and that noise dominates total MSE.

L_2-optimal allocation pulls bins out of the dense interior and into the
tails: ∝ p(x)^{1/3} is much flatter than ∝ p(x).

## Cost

The user's "sort and slice" is O(N log N) with a sort, or O(N) per pass
through a streaming quantile-sketch. Lloyd-Max is more iteration-heavy:
each iteration is O(N + K), and we do ~10-30 iterations. In practice we
build a coarse log-spaced fine-bin histogram first (one O(N) pass), then
run Lloyd-Max on the histogram (cheap, O(K · iters)). Total cost similar
to the naive scheme.

## Caveats

- LMQ is L_2-optimal; for our NMAE eval (L_1) the asymptotically right
  thing is k-medians (L_1-optimal centroids). The codec actually saves
  both L_2 means and L_1 medians as alternative recon points, but we use
  the L_2 means at eval time. Switching could shave another small fraction
  off MAE. Hasn't been tried.
- The "equal-mass + mean centroids" comparison above approximates the
  empirical distribution by treating LMQ recon points as the fine grid;
  the absolute numbers are slightly biased toward LMQ. The directional
  result (LMQ ≫ equal-mass for L_2) is robust.
